Principles of microarray

Principles of Microarray Technology

Microarrays allow simultaneous measurement of thousands of RNA transcripts by hybridizing labeled cDNA to probes fixed on a solid surface.

  • Probes: Short DNA sequences (~25‚Äì70 bp) immobilized on a glass slide or chip.
  • Hybridization: Labeled sample cDNA binds to complementary probes.
  • Detection: Fluorescent intensity at each spot corresponds to transcript abundance.

Defining probes (again)

In the context of microarray, a probe is a short, single-stranded DNA sequence immobilized on the array surface that specifically hybridizes to a complementary target RNA (or cDNA) sequence, enabling the detection and quantification of gene expression levels.

Microarray Workflow Overview

  1. RNA extraction from control and test samples
  2. Reverse transcription to cDNA with fluorescent labeling
  3. Hybridization of cDNA to complementary probes on chip
  4. Washing to remove unbound cDNA
  5. Scanning to measure fluorescence
  6. Normalization and analysis of intensity data

One or two color arrays?

A major design consideration in a microarray experiment is whether to measure the expression levels from each sample on separate microarrays (one-colour array) or to compare relative expression levels between a pair of samples on a single microarray (two-colour array). The overall performance of one-colour and two-colour arrays is similar.

Source: EBI functional genomics courses

One-Color vs Two-Color Systems

üü© One-Color (Single Channel)

  • Each sample hybridized to its own array.
  • Common in Affymetrix and Illumina platforms.
  • Signal intensity represents abs. expression for one sample.

    • Example: Control = Cy3 (green), Treated = Cy5 (red)

Normalization

Normalization method

Normalization is the process of adjusting microarray data to remove systematic technical variation that is not related to biological differences. Most known and used method is RMA Normalization (Robust Multi-array Average).

RMA Normalization

Purpose: To correct background noise and make expression values comparable across microarrays.

Main Steps:

  • Background correction ‚Äì adjusts for optical noise and nonspecific signal.
  • Quantile normalization ‚Äì forces all arrays to have the same overall distribution. You can possibly use only this as a normalization method.
  • Summarization ‚Äì combines probe-level data into one expression value per gene using a median polish model.

RMA Normalization

Advantages:

  • Reduces array-to-array variability.
  • Improves reproducibility in downstream differential expression analysis.
  • Implemented in tools like affy and oligo (R/Bioconductor).

Statistical analysis

Fold Change in Microarray Experiments

Fold change quantifies the magnitude of gene expression difference between two conditions (e.g., treated vs. control).

Fold Change = Mean Expression (treated) / Mean Expression (control)

To symmetrize up- and down-regulation: log2(Fold Change) ‚Üí +1 = 2√ó up, -1 = 2√ó down

Fold change alone does not indicate statistical significance ‚Äî it measures effect size only.

p-Value and Multiple Testing

p-value: probability of observing a difference as extreme as the measured one, assuming no true difference (null hypothesis). In microarrays, we test thousands of genes simultaneously ‚Üí many false positives by chance.

Need multiple testing correction to control for these errors:

  • Bonferroni correction: very strict, divides Œ± by number of tests.
  • Benjamini‚ÄìHochberg (FDR): controls expected false discovery rate; widely used in microarray and RNA-seq.

limma in Microarray Analysis

limma (Linear Models for Microarray Data) is one of the most widely used R packages for analyzing microarray (and RNA-seq) data. It provides a robust framework for identifying differentially expressed genes using linear models and empirical Bayes statistics.

Nanostring (2003)

Nanostring

Microarray: Relies on hybridization where labeled sample nucleic acids bind to complementary sequences on a solid surface. The amount of binding is then measured by fluorescence, which is proportional to the amount of target RNA. This method requires amplification steps.

NanoString: Uses a direct-counting, amplification-free method. It attaches fluorescent ‚Äúreporter‚Äù probes to the target nucleic acids, which are then counted directly, providing a more precise count of the original molecules.

Both of them use same differential gene expression methods.
